# Chapter 16: Caring for Residents with Dementia and Alzheimer's

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:
- Define dementia and Alzheimer's disease
- Recognize common behavioral and emotional changes
- Use effective communication strategies
- Prevent wandering and ensure safety
- Manage challenging behaviors with compassion
- Support both residents and families

---

## 16.1 Understanding Dementia

**Dementia** is a term describing a group of symptoms affecting thinking, memory, and social ability. It's not a normal part of aging and is caused by disease or injury to brain cells.

### What Dementia Is NOT:
- Not a single disease
- Not necessarily part of aging
- Not something the person can "snap out of"
- Not stupidity or laziness
- Not something the person is doing on purpose

### Common Causes of Dementia:
- **Alzheimer's Disease** (most common, 60-80% of cases)
- **Vascular Dementia** (from small strokes)
- **Lewy Body Dementia** (protein deposits in brain)
- **Frontotemporal Dementia** (affects personality and behavior)
- **Mixed Dementia** (combination of types)

### Stages of Dementia:

**Early Stage (Mild):**
- Memory loss starts
- Difficulty with complex tasks
- May hide problems
- Knows something is wrong
- May still drive
- Awareness of diagnosis

**Middle Stage (Moderate - Longest Stage):**
- Increased memory loss
- Confusion about time/place
- Behavioral changes
- Cannot live alone safely
- Needs help with ADLs
- May not recognize family

**Late Stage (Severe):**
- Loss of physical abilities
- Cannot communicate verbally
- Complete dependence on care
- May not recognize anyone
- Needs 24-hour supervision
- May need bed care

## 16.2 Behavioral Symptoms and What They Mean

Dementia can cause many behavioral changes. Remember: **behavior is communication**. The resident is trying to tell you something.

### Common Challenging Behaviors:

**Repetitive Questions or Stories:**
- Why they do it: Short-term memory loss; it feels like the first time
- How to respond: Answer calmly, as if new; don't correct or argue
- What helps: Write information on a card they can see; use photos

**Wandering:**
- Why they do it: Seeking something familiar; following old routines; escaping stress
- How to respond: Stay calm; don't chase; redirect gently
- What helps: Safe areas to walk; ID bracelets; familiar objects

**Aggression or Verbal Outbursts:**
- Why they do it: Fear; pain; frustration; feeling threatened or rushed
- How to respond: Stay calm and safe; don't take it personally; step back
- What helps: Reduce triggers; give space; approach slowly

**Sundowning (increased confusion/agitation at evening):**
- Why they do it: Fatigue; reduced light; overstimulation; medication effects
- How to respond: Calm environment; familiar activities; safety measures
- What helps: Routine; gentle music; adequate lighting; reduced noise

**Inappropriate Sexual Behavior:**
- Why they do it: Confusion; searching for comfort; not remembering appropriate boundaries
- How to respond: Calmly redirect; don't shame; ensure safety
- What helps: Avoid triggers; redirect; distraction; same-gender caregiver if possible

**Refusal of Care:**
- Why they do it: Fear; not understanding; feeling rushed; pain
- How to respond: Stop; reassess; give choices; come back later
- What helps: Calm approach; simple explanations; respect; patience

**Accusatory Behavior:**
- Why they do it: Confusion; lost items; not remembering where they put things
- How to respond: Listen; don't argue; search together; reassure
- What helps: Simplify environment; secure valuables; gentle responses

## 16.3 Communication Strategies

Effective communication is the foundation of dementia care. It shows respect and reduces behavioral issues.

### General Principles:

**SPEAK:**
- **S**lowly – Give time to process
- **P**eacefully – Calm tone and body language
- **E**ye contact – Face the person
- **A**ctively listen – Really hear them
- **K**indly – Show warmth and respect

### Specific Techniques:

**1. Use Simple Language**
- Short sentences (5 words or fewer when possible)
- One idea at a time
- Use concrete words instead of abstract
- Avoid explaining reasons

**Poor:** "Mrs. Chen, I know you're upset about leaving the day program, but transportation will be here in 15 minutes to take you back to your home, which is where you live now."  
**Better:** "Time to go home now. Your ride is here."

**2. Avoid Arguing or Correcting**
- Arguing makes them more upset and confused
- They won't "win" the argument; dementia prevents reasoning
- Even if they're wrong, forcing facts causes distress

**Poor:** "Mom, you already ate lunch. You ate chicken and rice."  
**Better:** "Are you hungry? Let me get you a snack."

**3. Use Validation**
- Acknowledge their feelings, even if the facts are wrong
- Meet them in their reality (sometimes)
- Show empathy and respect

**Poor:** "No, your mother can't visit. She died 20 years ago."  
**Better:** "You miss your mother. Tell me what she was like."

**4. Redirect and Distract**
- Move attention to something positive
- Use their interests
- Change the subject or activity
- Don't say "don't"—say what TO do instead

**Poor:** "Stop asking about lunch."  
**Better:** "Let's look at these photos together."

**5. Use Gestures and Touch**
- Words may not work; use non-verbal communication
- Demonstrate what you want
- Hold out a hand and smile
- Appropriate gentle touch comforts many

**6. Present Choices**
- Offer 2-3 simple options
- Helps them feel in control
- Avoid yes/no questions (often triggers refusal)

**Poor:** "Do you want to bathe now?"  
**Better:** "Would you like a shower or bath?"

**7. Use Photos and Memory Cues**
- Photos of familiar people and places
- Music from their era
- Objects connected to memories
- These can calm and engage them

## 16.4 Activities and Engagement

Activities support mental health, provide purpose, and reduce behavioral issues.

### Benefits of Activities:
- Engagement and enjoyment
- Maintains function and mobility
- Reduces depression and anxiety
- Decreases behavioral problems
- Improves sleep
- Strengthens relationships

### Choosing Activities:

**Adapt to abilities:**
- Early stage: Complex activities, outings, volunteering
- Middle stage: Simpler activities, familiar hobbies
- Late stage: Sensory stimulation, music, touch, familiar items

**Consider interests:**
- Ask family about former hobbies
- What did they enjoy doing?
- What were their professions?
- What made them happy?

### Activity Ideas:

**Physical Activities:**
- Walking and light exercise
- Gentle dancing
- Gardening (if safe)
- Yard time
- Tossing a ball

**Cognitive Activities:**
- Simple puzzles (large pieces)
- Sorting activities
- Card games (simple ones)
- Bingo
- Reading simple books

**Creative Activities:**
- Coloring with large crayons
- Painting
- Music playing or listening
- Singing
- Crafts with supervision

**Social Activities:**
- Games with others
- Group meals
- Visits from family
- Pet visits
- Group outings

**Sensory Activities:**
- Music therapy
- Aromatherapy
- Hand massage
- Textured items to touch
- Favorite foods to taste

### Activity Timing:
- Schedule during calm times of day
- Avoid during sundowning if possible
- Use activities to redirect behavior
- Keep sessions short (15-30 minutes)
- Stop if the person becomes frustrated

## 16.5 Managing Wandering and Safety

Wandering is common in dementia. The goal is **safe, supervised wandering**, not restriction.

### Why Residents Wander:
- Looking for familiar places or people
- Following old habits or routines
- Seeking activity or stimulation
- Need for exercise
- Confusion about location
- Searching for the bathroom

### Preventing Unsafe Wandering:

**Environmental Modifications:**
- Secure exits (alarms or locks)
- Fencing for outdoor areas
- Adequate lighting throughout
- Remove obstacles
- Remove dangerous items
- Visual cues (signs, colors)
- Locked storage for meds/supplies

**Identification and Tracking:**
- ID bracelets with name/facility info
- Emergency contact information
- Photos for staff
- GPS devices (if facility allows)
- Clothing with ID

**Supervision:**
- Check on resident frequently
- Assign staff to monitor
- One-on-one supervision if needed
- Doors alarmed if wandering risk

**Engagement:**
- Keep resident active and engaged
- Provide secure walking areas
- Use activities and routines
- Maintain familiar environment
- Regular exercise

**Communication:**
- Alert all staff to wandering risk
- Inform security and maintenance
- Keep doors labeled clearly
- Tell family about safety measures
- Post info in common areas

## 16.6 Sexuality and Intimacy in Dementia Care

Residents with dementia may express sexual needs, which can make CNAs uncomfortable. Handle professionally and respectfully.

### Understanding Sexual Behavior in Dementia:

**Why it happens:**
- Need for comfort and reassurance
- Not remembering inappropriate boundaries
- Confusion about person (may confuse caregiver with spouse)
- Seeking familiar experiences
- Response to stimulation

### Appropriate Responses:

**DO:**
- Remain calm and professional
- Redirect to appropriate activity
- Maintain dignity
- Protect the resident's privacy
- Inform nurse of behavior
- Use same-gender caregiver if possible
- Ensure no harm or unwanted contact

**DON'T:**
- Shame or scold the person
- Punish or ignore
- Assume they understand boundaries
- Leave them unobserved
- Discuss behavior with other residents
- Ignore repeated behavior

### Prevention:
- Appropriate supervision
- Limit trigger situations
- Redirect early
- Keep person engaged
- Maintain dignity while setting boundaries

## 16.7 Working with Family and Caregivers

Family members may have complicated feelings about their loved one's dementia.

### Common Family Challenges:
- Grief for the person they knew
- Guilt about placement or decisions
- Frustration with behavioral changes
- Difficulty accepting decline
- Struggling to know how to help

### Supporting Families:

**Communication:**
- Keep family informed of changes
- Share activity successes
- Listen to their concerns
- Explain dementia-related changes
- Don't make them feel judged

**Guidance:**
- Show them how to communicate
- Suggest visit activities
- Explain why behaviors happen
- Give tips for management
- Validate their feelings

**Boundaries:**
- Professional relationships
- Follow facility policies
- Don't promise "cures"
- Support facility care plan
- Refer major concerns to nurse

### Your Role in Family Involvement:
- Greet family warmly
- Share positive observations
- Facilitate visits
- Help create meaningful interactions
- Report family concerns to nurse
- Support the resident during visits

## 16.8 Caring for Yourself

Dementia care is emotionally and physically demanding.

### Common Caregiver Challenges:
- Emotional exhaustion
- Compassion fatigue
- Frustration with repetitive behaviors
- Feeling helpless
- Grief over decline

### Self-Care Strategies:
- Take breaks when needed
- Talk with colleagues
- Use facility support resources
- Exercise and eat well
- Maintain outside interests
- Accept that dementia is progressive
- Celebrate small successes
- Remember the person, not just the disease

### Keeping Perspective:
- The resident isn't doing these things ON PURPOSE
- The behavior is the disease, not the person
- Your presence and care matter, even if they don't remember
- Compassion prevents burnout
- Small moments of connection are valuable

---

## Key Takeaways

✓ Dementia is progressive brain disease, not normal aging  
✓ Behavior is communication—listen for unmet needs  
✓ Validation and redirection work better than correction  
✓ Keep communication simple and calm  
✓ Adapt activities to ability level  
✓ Provide safe environments to prevent unsafe wandering  
✓ Treat residents with full respect and dignity  
✓ Support families and take care of yourself
