# Chapter 1: Role and Responsibilities of the CNA

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:
- Define the scope of practice for CNAs in Texas
- Describe the chain of command in a healthcare facility
- Explain the importance of observation and reporting
- Document care appropriately and accurately
- Function effectively as part of a healthcare team

---

## 1.1 What is a Nurse Aide?

A **Certified Nursing Aide (CNA)** is a healthcare professional trained to provide basic care and support to patients and residents in hospitals, skilled nursing facilities, and other healthcare settings. In Texas, CNAs work under the supervision of a registered nurse (RN) or licensed vocational nurse (LVN).

### Key Responsibilities:
- Assist residents with activities of daily living (ADLs)
- Maintain a clean, safe environment
- Observe and report changes in resident condition
- Provide emotional support and companionship
- Follow the care plan established by the nurse
- Communicate effectively with the healthcare team

### Why This Matters:
CNAs are often the primary caregivers, spending the most time with residents. Your actions, observations, and attitude directly impact resident comfort, safety, and recovery.

---

## 1.2 Working Under Supervision

**The most important principle to remember:** CNAs do not work independently. You work **under the supervision and direction of licensed nurses**.

### What This Means:
- The nurse is responsible for your actions
- You follow the care plan and specific instructions from the nurse
- You report findings to the nurse, not to other CNAs
- You ask for help when unsure about any task
- You **never** make decisions about resident care

### The Chain of Command:
```
Resident/Family
    ↓
Nurse Supervisor (RN/LVN)
    ↓
Registered Nurse or Licensed Vocational Nurse
    ↓
Certified Nursing Aide
    ↓
Support Services
```

### Always Remember:
- Report directly to your nurse supervisor
- If your nurse is busy, wait politely or inform their supervisor
- Never skip the chain of command
- Document serious changes and report them immediately

## 1.3 Observation and Reporting

One of your most important duties is **careful observation**. You see residents most frequently and often notice changes before anyone else.

### What to Observe:
- **Physical changes:** skin color, temperature, swelling, wounds
- **Behavioral changes:** mood, speech, confusion, agitation
- **Functional changes:** weakness, difficulty walking, balance problems
- **Elimination:** color, amount, frequency of urine or stool
- **Appetite and hydration:** intake, refusal of food or water
- **Pain:** location, severity, what makes it better or worse
- **Vital signs:** temperature, pulse, respiration, blood pressure

### How to Report Effectively:

**Use OBJECTIVE language** - report what you see/hear/smell, not opinions:

**Poor:** "Mrs. Lee is acting weird today."  
**Better:** "Mrs. Lee is confused; she doesn't recognize her daughter and called me by the wrong name."

**Poor:** "The resident seems unhappy."  
**Better:** "The resident has been tearful and refused breakfast and lunch."

### Reporting Process:
1. Inform your nurse supervisor immediately
2. Use objective language and specific details
3. Provide timing: exactly when you noticed the change
4. Document in the chart as instructed
5. Continue monitoring and update the nurse if the situation changes

---

## 1.4 Documentation and Charting

Documentation is the legal record of care provided. Accurate charting is essential for:
- Continuity of care
- Legal protection
- Monitoring resident progress
- Communicating among the healthcare team

### Documentation Guidelines:

**DO:**
- Document immediately after care
- Record exact observations (not estimates)
- Use objective, factual language
- Include date, time, and your signature
- Document what you actually saw/did
- Note abnormal findings

**DON'T:**
- Chart before care is completed
- Use opinions or judgments
- Leave blank spaces
- Erase or white-out entries
- Chart for another CNA
- Use abbreviations not approved by the facility

### Example Documentation:
```
7/15/2024 - 2:30 PM - Assisted Mr. Jackson with bath.
Resident alert and oriented x3. Skin intact, no redness noted.
Expressed pain level 3/10 in left shoulder during bathing.
Resident ate 75% of lunch tray. Drank 8 oz of water.
No unusual findings noted. -CNA Smith
```

## 1.5 Working as a Team Member

Healthcare is a **team effort**. Everyone—nurses, doctors, therapists, dietary staff, housekeeping, and CNAs—has an important role.

### Team Communication:
- Communicate clearly and respectfully
- Ask questions when unsure
- Share observations that help care
- Respect others' expertise and time
- Be willing to help colleagues
- Participate in team meetings when invited

### Professional Behavior:
- Arrive on time and ready to work
- Follow facility policies
- Wear clean, professional uniforms
- Use appropriate language
- Maintain confidentiality
- Show respect to all team members
- Take responsibility for your actions

### Handling Conflict:
- Speak respectfully to the person involved
- Focus on the issue, not personalities
- Listen to the other perspective
- Ask for help from a supervisor if needed
- Never vent to residents or their families

---

## 1.6 Professional Development

As a CNA, your professional growth benefits residents and your career.

### Ongoing Learning:
- Complete required in-service training annually
- Attend workshops on new procedures or conditions
- Learn from experienced colleagues
- Stay updated on facility policies
- Continue education toward RN/LVN if desired

### Self-Care:
- Manage stress through exercise and rest
- Seek support when experiencing burnout
- Follow safety protocols to prevent injury
- Maintain your physical and mental health
- Remember: you cannot care for others if you don't care for yourself

---

## Key Takeaways

✓ CNAs provide essential direct care and observation under nurse supervision  
✓ Your observations are vital—report changes quickly and accurately  
✓ Documentation must be objective, timely, and complete  
✓ You work as part of a healthcare team with a clear chain of command  
✓ Professional behavior and communication support quality care  
✓ Never go beyond your scope or act independently
